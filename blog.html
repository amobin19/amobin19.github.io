#1 The Major Decision

Electrical Engineering (EE) vs. Computer Science (CS) vs. Computer Engineering (CPE)

Hi everyone! Welcome back! If you're back here reading this again, that probably means that you had a even the tiniest bit of amusement from this blog. Which is amazing. You're amazing. So as you can see, this is a pretty informal blog. I want to keep the vibe as mellow and chilled out as possible. Some information you should know about this site. I developed it. I'm not using some online software blog tool like Wix or Wordpress. Everything that you see infront of you I created from scratch using web development tools like HTML, CSS, JS, etc. I haven't done anything with the back end yet. If you don't know what that is, it's basically how information is saved on a website. Front-end is basically the development of static web pages without saving information in a nutshell. I'll do a front-end/back-end post later on. I'll develop the site more as summer approaches and hopefully add more functionality such as user comments, forums, make the site more pretty etc.

So for today's topic I'm going to expand more upon my major and hopefully answer the question I hear a lot "what's the difference between computer engineering and computer science and electrical engineering?"

I'm gonna start this by explaining a theoretical spectrum which goes from bottom up. When I'm explaining this, think of your desktop, laptop, or cell phone. All three of these items are made up of multiple components but are all made up of the physical components (the hardware) which powers your device and runs applications (software) which adds functionality to your device. In simplest terms EE majors focus their learning on the hardware (the lower level of the spectrum), designing devices which power your devices. CS majors focus their learning on the software (higher level of the spectrum), designing applications which your device runs. CPE majors focus their learning on the area in between. CPE majors are responsible for understanding the integration between the hardware and software and designing technology which can effectively combine the two. This requires an understanding of both hardware and software. For that reason, the CPE curriculum at UVA requires students to take classes in both the EE department and the CS department.

Now you might be thinking "why can't everyone do CPE if it requires the knowledge of both hardware and software?" My response to that would be that as a CPE major I have a good fundamental understanding of each area that builds a computer, but I lack a general expertise that a CS major or an EE major has in their respective fields. The reason I have selected this major is because it allows me to explore all areas of building a computer system and I can focus my learning on anything that I am specifically interested in.

Coming into UVA, I always thought that I'd be an EE major. My philosophy was that "you can't have software without the hardware to run it on." However I learned that I enjoyed developing software and I liked visually seeing my work through the usage of software applications. The reason I did not do just pure CS is because I still wanted to have the ability to understand the lower level hardware design. I have the ability, should I choose to do so, develop hardware or software (whatever I'm passionate about) in my future. I love having that option in my hands rather than being constrained to one specific field.

Being a CPE major I have enjoyed being able to speak with EE students as well as CS students. I enjoyed having the option through this major to decide what I'm passionate about. I've had my downs through my electronics, circuit analysis, and OS classes but I've also had a lot of ups through my Web App Development and Robotics classes.

I hope this post helped you all learn a bit more on the differences between the 3 majors. If you take anything away from this, know that you can be successful in any of the 3 majors. If you are passionate about physically building the technologies, then EE might be your passion. If you are passionate about building applications, then CS might be your path. If you are unsure like me, but are interested in computer technologies, then CPE might be your field. Just know that opportunites are out there for all 3 of these and not any single one of these are better than the other.

Thanks for reading this if you have! Hope that you have learned something from this, whether it might be about me or about the differences/similarites between the majors. Till next time!

#2 What's Clouding Your Mind?

What is the “cloud”? How did I get into it?

Hello people of Earth and beyond! Today I hope to enlighten you all with a little brain dump on what the “cloud” really is. Before I get started, I wanna apologize for the huge gap since my last post. Lets just say some things like college graduation, a new career, and an epic pandemic got in the way of me writing posts since my last one. Hopefully I can keep this momentum going.

Anyway, let’s get to it! I’d like to think that I’m at least somewhat qualified to be talking about this topic today as fortunately I have been able to successfully achieve 2 AWS certifications to this point (I’ll talk more about what AWS is as you keep reading). I’d like to kick things off with how I even got involved and interested in this topic of the “cloud” in the first place with a little anecdote.

There I was, sitting in the library at the University of Virginia with possibly the worst class enrollment time for the coming semester. There’s been a buzz going around grounds that “Cloud Computing” with Professor Humphrey is a must for students in the CS department. I’m gonna cut straight to the chase, my enrollment time was possibly the last one and I did not get into the class. I think I was position 120 something on the waitlist for a class of 80. So yeah, I was bummed since I was about to enter my final semester of undergrad and I didn’t get into the class I wanted.

I think not getting into the class was the pivot moment for me in the start of my young cloud journey. I did a little bit of research into the syllabus of the class, thinking what’s all the buzz about. Was it an easy A or what? I started looking through the curriculum and I was amazed at all the different opportunities there are with this technology and just from one look at the course syllabus I knew that this was something that gauged my interest. I knew at that point that I could try to learn these things on my own and be a cloud enthusiast.

So what is the cloud you may ask? The easy answer is to look up at the sky and you see those cotton-ball, poofy looking things? That’s a cloud. End of post...nope! That’s A cloud but it's not the type of cloud I’m talking about. In as basic terms as possible, the cloud is a bunch of servers located at data centers that can be accessed over the internet. You might follow up by asking, “what’s so cool about that?” Well think of it like this, how does it feel knowing that you have almost used up all of your storage on your phone? It makes it more difficult to readily access applications, take more photos, text your loved ones, or even play games. At that point you might be considering getting a new phone. All of that because the storage on your phone is almost at max occupancy. That's where the cloud comes in.

Do you ever get those notifications that say to upload your data into the cloud and then all of a sudden a bunch of space will free up on your device? If you do that, you’re backing up and storing all of your data in the “cloud” which frees up space on your device. You’re not losing your information, rather you have an alternative storage space. If you get a new phone, you can easily restore your new device with the same features from your old device by retrieving it from the cloud. Think of any social media for that matter, how is it possible for you to access your Facebook from any device? It’s because your Facebook account is stored and managed in the cloud. That’s really what the cloud is, a massive storage unit to save information.

What’s AWS? AWS is known as Amazon Web Services. It’s Amazon’s version of the cloud. It is also one of the largest cloud providers in the world. Lots of companies such as Facebook, LinkedIn, and Netflix all use AWS to store their data. Imagine any of these companies being responsible for their own storage based on the number of people that use their services. They would need to have loads of offices just for servers to hold their customer data. Instead of being responsible for their own storage units, AWS comes in to provide that space for them.

Since its birth in 2002, AWS has grown to become known as the Operating System of the internet. It has a lot more capabilities than to simply store your data. On the cloud now, individuals and companies have the ability to run their technology applications on top of the base platform that is provided for them. Since then, companies such as Google, Microsoft, and IBM have all begun to develop their own cloud platforms. The giant of them all right now is still AWS. That’s why I have chosen to pursue some of those certifications so I could explore the cloud giant that is the OS of the internet in the modern era.

I can’t tell you what’s gonna happen in the future. But I can tell you that cloud technology is only going to grow as more and more companies slowly move their infrastructure from their own physical servers to that of cloud servers provided by companies like AWS.

#3 A Day in the Life

What does my typical day look like?

Hey everyone! Welcome back to another blog post. Glad to have y’all keep reading these. So for today I thought I’d change it up a bit and do “A Day in the Life” post about my day as a Software Engineer at Freddie Mac. I’m feeling pretty nostalgic working from home and missing my time at the office pre-COVID19 so I thought why not write about it and give everyone a little peek into my world. So here we go!

I normally wake up around 7:30am every morning, wash-up, and get ready for work. Dress code at work as a developer is pretty chill so I get by wearing jeans. I try to keep the top looking fresh so it’s always a collar of some sort whether it's a long-sleeve button down or a polo. Then I put on my shoes and generally try to leave my home around 8am. The goal is to get to work by 9am and commute is give or take 45min, so it works out.

People don’t give their commute as much attention as they should but I personally love commuting to work. Sometimes I change it up whether it's taking my car or taking public transportation, such as the metro. My company has a pretty nice public transportation benefit so I try to use that from time to time. If I really need to haul to work I take my car. Commuting to work is a blast because I’m pretty big on podcasts. So I always pop in one of my top podcasts on the way to work and on the way back. On the way to work it’s normally some type of news whether it's the NY Times the Daily, Wall Street Journal, or some tech podcast such as the Accidental Tech Podcast. This gives me some talking points at work with colleagues and just generally informs me on what’s going on around the world.

I get to work around 9ish and go to my office. I set up my laptop on my docking station and normally the first thing I do is go to the cafeteria for some breakfast by myself or with colleagues if they’re interested. On a good day my go-to breakfast at the cafeteria are Freddie’s freshly made homefries, scrambled eggs, and an everything bagel. Then I normally check my email and make sure I have everything set. My workday normally kicks off from standup meetings at 10am where I give general updates on my progression, what I’m going to be working on, and any roadblocks I might have.

I’m on a Modern Delivery team, in short it’s a form of Agile Software Development. You know what? I’ll create another post just to explain Agile, but basically standups are one of the meetings that all Agile teams have as part of their sprints. Sprints are basically the time period that the teams deliver their tasks by. Right after standup until around lunch I’m working on those tasks and project work, in other words I’m coding! In between standup and lunch we may have meetings such as meetings for refining tasks for later sprints, knowledge transfer meetings, or I’m getting assistance from a colleague if I’m stuck.

Then lunch is the next milestone for the day. Sometimes I pack, sometimes I go to the cafeteria, other times the other associates from my college hire program and I go out for lunch. We normally try to go out on Fridays to celebrate the end of the week, ya know small victories! If I’m packing it might be leftovers from the night before, if it's at the cafeteria my go-to is always the buffalo chicken wrap, and when we go out we normally try to change it up based on whatever we’re feeling.

Normally I get done with lunch around 1pm. From 1pm-3pm, back to work. Again may have some more meetings scattered through this time but mainly its project work. 3pm I try to stretch my legs, go for a walk, or catch up with colleagues. Then from 3:15pm-5pm, closing out the day with more project work.

Once 5pm rolls around I pack up and get ready for the commute home, unless I have a work happy hour or other plans but typically I’m going home. It’s commute time, so you know what that means...more podcasts! On my way home I normally listen to sports related podcasts. Whether it's Stephen A. Smith going off on First Take or Shannon Sharpe and Skip Bayless on Undisputed. I get my daily dosage of sports news from my commute home.

I get home from work around 6pm. I then go to the local gym in my neighborhood. I workout for about an hour or so then come home, shower, and eat dinner. After all of that it’s probably anywhere from 8pm-9pm where I have time to myself before I go to bed. In that time I could be doing a variety of things. I may catch up with friends, I may catch up on tv shows, play video games, or I may try to be productive and work on some side projects like this blog! By midnight I try to get to bed.

There ya have it folks, a day in the life of a SWE from my point of view. I’m not sure how useful this post was but I just thought I’d give a rundown in case anyone was curious to know what I really do at work and how my day normally goes. I’m sure it’s not too different from that of others in my field so hope this gave some insight. Thanks for reading, till next time!

#4 Silicon Valley, the show...not the place!

My (SPOILER FREE) review of the first season of the show Silicon Valley!

Hey all! Hope everyone is doing well during these times and staying healthy. For today’s blog I thought I’d change it up a bit and do a blog where I review and give my opinion on the first season of HBO’s original show, Silicon Valley. For those of you who are unfamiliar, the show began in 2014 and is about a 26 year old who tries to build a company around a product which he started at...you guessed it, Silicon Valley! The reason I want to do this blog, and I probably will for the rest of the seasons too, is because the show depicts technology from a startup culture perspective and from an entrepreneurial mindset detailing how a product goes into market.

So far I love this show for a couple of reasons. The first is because I am a tech geek at heart. I wouldn’t be surprised if you don’t feel the same way about this show if you’re not a true geek who loves technology. The main product the show is based around is a compression algorithm which basically condenses any file that is processed to allow maximum data utility and usage. If that is a bunch of gibberish to you, then there is a chance you won’t relate with the show. Also a lot of the lingo used and the main ideas surrounding the plot of the show is based around computer technology. There are also a lot of references to real tech companies and there is a lot of witty humor spread through the show. If what I’m saying so far is something that sounds super cool to you, then you should really consider watching this show.

The other reason I love this show is because of its entrepreneurial take in product development. Having a Minor in Technology Entrepreneurship, I was able to see how a lot of what I was taught in school is used throughout the show. The show does a phenomenal job depicting the steps necessary to turn a product into a company. The show starts with the minimum viable product (MVP) already developed. Which is funny since the first episode is titled "Minimum Viable Product". With the MVP developed the show takes the viewers on a journey into the steps that come after the initial development such as pitching the idea to potential investors, creating a business plan, managing finances, maintaining team culture, and defending the product against fierce competition. Having worked in a startup as part of one of my internships, I certainly see how all of those factors and more are important. It’s really interesting to see it all even if it is for televised entertainment.

With all that being said, the show is not perfect. The challenges that Richard, the protagonist, faced with his team are more likely to be incredibly more challenging in the real world. For example what he did in the final episode of the season seems like it would take way more than one night to complete especially on your own. Receiving funding from your first pitch, having venture capitalists ready to invest millions on your product after a single successful demo, and growing the company at the rate that it is in the show all seem to be more fictional than it would be in reality. Then again, this is for television entertainment so you have to think of it from that perspective when watching.

To close off this blog, if you are interested to see what it is like to get a product going in the epicenter of innovation and you just generally want some television entertainment, then I would recommend watching Silicon Valley on HBO. You will definitely get a good laugh in and get a perspective into the fictional lives of innovators in the valley.

#5 The Social Dilemma

How social media companies run? What are consequences that society faces from these applications? Review of the documentary The Social Dilemma.

Hello, world! Hope everyone is doing alright. A lot of what I’m about to say next are some logistics so if you want to keep reading feel free or go ahead and skip to the crux of the blog from the paragraph after.

I apologize for not having written one of these in a while. Work has been pretty crazy these past couple of weeks as we have some releases coming up. If you want to know what my typical day-to-day looks like (non-quarantine edition), checkout blog #3! I also picked up the AWS SysOps Administrator Associate Certificate during that time as well. Checkout blog #2 if you want to learn more about AWS and cloud computing! All in all, the last several weeks have been busy. Some more household items quickly, I know I said I would continue to review the show Silicon Valley when I wrote blog #4. However after completing the show, a lot of what I said in that blog basically covers what I would be writing about. So to reduce redundancy, I won’t write another one about that. Without further ado, here’s blog #5!

Society today has a problem. Technology. I know that sounds crazy, especially coming from me, a technology enthusiast. However in order to truly appreciate something, it is important to understand its flaws. Think for a second, how often you are looking into a screen. If you’re on a smartphone check your screen time activity. Technology is consuming the way we live and interact with people which in turn has a domino effect into other problems that are faced around the globe today. Tristan Harris, an ex-Google employee and founder of the Center for Humane Technology, has said that “billions of people give themselves to the phone thinking they're superior, but the platform has you in their grip.” Now think about that, you are giving so much of your time to something as sophisticated as your phone, more specifically social media. I’m going to use Facebook as the primary example due to its general acclaim. This software processes almost 500 terabytes of data each day. To put that into perspective that’s 2,700 million shares of likes and 300 million photos per day.

Before I get into how and why social media companies like Facebook generate that much data, I want to discuss the product development lifecycle. This was taught to me by some of my entrepreneurial, business classes at UVA. There are many forms to this process but basically it has four major steps in this order: Entry, Growth, Stability, and Transition. I’m not going to talk about this in detail but before you continue reading I think it’s important that you have an understanding that this is the skeleton of how companies run their businesses. The Entry phase is where innovators and engineers identify a problem and work to find a solution. After coming up with an initial solution, the innovators have to constantly test and ensure that the product or service that they came up with is viable. If it’s not, they have to keep adjusting until they have something that works. Once they do, they enter the Growth stage. As part of this stage, the sole purpose is to keep building off of the customer base that the product or service is geared toward. Innovators in this stage are not focused on the product or service as much as they are on growing the customer base with marketing and advertising strategies. Then comes the Stability phase. In this phase innovators have a defined customer base which is growing at its own rate. The focus here is to maintain the customer. At this stage innovators should be thinking of ways to keep innovating the initial product to not lose customers. Next comes the Transition phase. In this stage companies improve upon their features or develop new features and cycle back to the early stages of this process or they hit a decline and withdraw their products or services.

This is the general business cycle, now keep this in mind as you keep reading. Now in terms of technology what is a user? The user of the technology is you. It is me. It is anyone that you know who uses a computer or technological service. Users normally have accounts to call their own where their activity is personalized to themselves. Think back to Facebook. How do you use it? You make an account. Now who do you know that's on Facebook? Probably everyone you know as the company has over 2.2 billion users around the globe. So at the end of the day we’re all users.

With over 2.2 billion users and over 500 terabytes of data processed each day, consider Facebook through the stages of the product development lifecycle. Mark Zuckerberg, founder and CEO, created Facebook as a platform to connect his peers at Harvard. Once he created the initial platform and it began to get recognition around the campus, he proceeded to the growth stage by hiring employees for a growing platform and continuing to expand to other schools and communities.

The next stage is where the crux of how companies like Facebook and other big tech companies make their revenue. Think about this, you are there when Facebook is inaugurated and you’re able to connect with your peers. But then what? What happens after you set up your profile and send/accept friend requests from your peers? Mark Zuckerberg, kept innovating. He and his colleagues created new features such as the like button, a public wall, news feed, etc to keep users constantly coming back for more. The new features that Facebook promotes constantly has the company proceeding through the product development lifecycle. The constant innovation that Facebook creates prevents the company from declining and makes them the biggest social media platform in the world. However through all this innovation there is a cost. It is the data that the users provide for them to use.

Social media companies like Facebook, have teams dedicated to developing algorithms to collect data and learn as much information about their users as they can. In brief an algorithm is a process to solve a problem. The problem that the process is solving is to learn as much about the users as possible. These algorithms are used in these applications to monitor your activity through the usage of the application. These companies monitor more than just your activity, they monitor segments such as how much time you spend on a page and how many times you go back to view a certain page. More data than that of what appears in your activity log are monitored.

They use the data with the intent of improving their applications to keep you, the user, coming back. Facebook might provide recommendations on what post to like, recommend videos to watch, or suggest friends for you to connect with. Every action you take, that action data is used to determine what to show you next to keep you coming back for more. Keeping users coming back for more and constantly staying in the product development lifecycle from the creation of features based on the data you provide are reasons why the “social dilemma” occurs.

The Netflix film, The Social Dilemma, is a documentary that educates its audience about the harms of technology, more specifically that of social media. This is not your average documentary by critics or general members of social media accounts. This movie is fully driven by the engineers who created the software themselves. The speakers in the documentary are ex-engineers at companies like Google, Facebook, Twitter, Uber, Pinterest, Apple, etc. These are companies that are the driving forces of the push in technological change and the economy.

I love the introductory hook of this documentary as these engineers introduce themselves. You know right off the bat that this documentary is going to be legit as it is led by the engineers of the applications themselves. You know that you will be getting an insight that you would not be getting from elsewhere. What gives it more credibility is that you know that they’re not going to be promoting their tech. Them providing a different angle to the software that they developed, is intriguing in itself.

This documentary spends the time discussing how social media companies that these engineers used to work for use the data that the users provide to constantly develop new features. Once that insight is provided it segways into the major problems that society faces with the constant development of the new features. By connecting people around the globe, providing features to express your best moments in life, receiving approval by getting likes and comments all build a facade of the user. Technology being more developed now than a generation ago is more accessible to young people such as kids and teenagers. They fall into this loop of constantly being available on these social media platforms. The tactics that these companies use to keep their users engaged lead to an addiction and a fake value of self based on engineered reaction features.

The film discusses how these applications create this fabricated value of the users which lead to severe consequences in society such as higher rates of depression and suicide, political movements, and the mass spread of falsified news. I remember vividly the day when Kobe Bryant died. My phone was blowing up with notifications from social media outlets. Twitter, Facebook, and Reddit were all blowing up with “news” of his death. I saw things such as Kobe Bryant and all of his daughters were in the crash or Kobe Bryant and his ex-teammate Rick Fox were in the crash. So many theories of what happened were being spread like wildfire, but what was the truth? Social media can create a domino effect and spread falsified information which in turn has its own consequences.

The big picture in this documentary is that the users of the technology are not in control. No matter how much they think that they are. Tristan Harris, also in the documentary, said that “we don't think of the technology as the existential threat. We give our lives to the technology thinking that it's going to be okay. However technology brings out the worst in society which is the existential threat. It creates loneliness and de-values you.”

I think that is a very powerful statement because I relate to it as I think most of you reading this should. I don’t consider technology as a threat but when I think of the consequences of constantly being on my phone or on the internet, I do realize the amount of control that it has over me. I also fall victim to technology and social media. It feels as if there’s no way of getting by without it. You could if you tried but the fact that even without it, you have a need to want to be on it is one of the effects of this generation and generations going forward. It wouldn’t be impossible to get by without it but the peer pressure that people would feel from seeing their network be connected gravitates people to use technology at their disposal.

Also to put it into perspective, sophisticated technology connected to the internet is really new relative to how long humans have been on this planet. Human generations before the current generation have interacted with others physically around them. Think about that now, humans have the capability to interact and express themselves to audiences greater than just the people around them. Adapting to that kind of exposure is really new and that process of that kind of exposure will have consequences as we evolve to it. Angles like these are what the engineers reflect upon and provide detail towards in the film.

I highly recommend that if you have the time, you check it out. It sheds a lot of light into a lot of things that we may not necessarily think about. Technology will forever be here and it does make information widely available for all of us. However, it is important for us to find a balance so we can maintain our humanity while improving the world we live in at the same time. That’s a wrap for this blog. I know it was a long one but hopefully you got something out of it or at least check out the film. Till next time yall!

P.S. - Check your Screen Time on your phone, you may be shocked!
